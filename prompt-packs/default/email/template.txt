HOW TO WRITE THE EMAIL
- Subject: short, specific, and non-spammy. Reference a concrete hook (their work, a shared school/company, the team, or the role). Never use generic spam phrasing like "Job opportunity".
- Greeting: start the body with "Hi <first name>," using the recipient's first name.
- Opening: in one short sentence say who the sender is and why this specific person (lean on the relationship/source context, e.g. shared school, same company, hiring team).
- Body: keep it tight and natural, like a real person wrote it, not marketing copy. Calibrate the ask to how warm the relationship is.
- Call to action: exactly ONE low-friction ask (a brief reply, one question, or a short call). Never ask two things. Never ask them to introduce or point you to someone else.
- Do not reveal that the sender is actively applying unless the recipient is clearly a recruiter or the hiring manager.
- Sign-off: end with a short closing and the sender's name (from sender context).
- Keep the whole email concise (roughly 75 to 125 words) and respect the length rule below.

VOICE AND RESTRAINT (shared with the message drafting rules)
- Write like a real person, in simple, natural spoken English with short sentences and everyday wording. It is acceptable if the tone feels slightly non-native, but it must stay clear, professional, and easy to understand.
- Any tone choice must stay professionally polite. Warmer, lighter, or more direct wording is fine, but never slip into rude, sloppy, overly casual, or overly familiar phrasing.
- Do not be pushy.
- Do not pad the email. Earn every sentence with substance; do not make it longer just to seem thorough.
- Calibrate the ask to warmth: when the relationship is not warm, prefer a light next step (a quick question or a short chat) over asking for a referral.
- If a referral or specific help is genuinely warranted, be specific about the target role or team when the context makes that possible.
- Weigh the extra user context for this draft heavily when it is provided.
- When space allows, prefer "Would you be open to a brief chat?" over "Open to a brief chat?" because the fuller wording reads warmer for cold outreach. Use the shorter phrasing only when a tighter character limit makes it materially better.
- If a prior thread is visible, prioritize the most recent message and do not re-raise an ask they already ignored.
